# Money Stories — Episode 1 Production Script (Kurzgesagt-Style Rework)

## EP #001: "The Invisible Machine That Made a Janitor Rich"

**Runtime target:** 10:30–11:00 · **VO word count:** ~1,620 (≈150 wpm)
**Narrator:** warm, curious, lightly amused explainer voice (present tense, second person —
NOT the Archivist; this rework retires the noir narrator for this format)
**Visual system:** flat vector animation, bold saturated colors on deep navy (#0a0f1e keeps the
brand), simple geometric characters, one recurring mascot (a small bird in a janitor's cap —
style-inspired by Kurzgesagt, not a copy of their birds), big readable numbers, scale zooms

### Style source (what we're borrowing from Kurzgesagt and why)

| Kurzgesagt pattern | How this script uses it |
|---|---|
| Viewer is the protagonist ("Imagine you…") | Cold open puts YOU next to the millionaire; Ledger closes on YOUR numbers |
| Counterintuitive declarative hook in first 15s | "There is a multimillionaire near you, disguised as a poor person" |
| One big invisible force explained via physical metaphor | Wealth = bathtub flows; compounding = self-replicating cells |
| Escalating scale / zoom-out | $55k → $450k → $1.6M → $8M ladder; 65 years compressed to seconds |
| Honest hedging ("this is oversimplified") | Model disclaimer said out loud, in-voice |
| A twist that complicates the neat story | Lehman goes to zero; then the darker twist — he over-saved |
| Optimistic nihilism ending | You control almost nothing — except the two levers that matter |
| Playful side-gags in visuals, never in the facts | Bird gags live in VISUAL directions only; figures stay sourced |

## PART 1 — "The Janitor" (0:45–2:30)

**[VISUAL: zoom into a tiny dot on a map of Vermont — Brattleboro. A farm, a dirt road,
a small bird-boy walking four miles to school, seasons flicking past like frames.]**

> **VO:** Meet Ronald Read. Born 1921 on a farm outside Brattleboro, Vermont. His family
> had almost nothing. He walked or hitchhiked about four miles to school and became the
> first person in his family to graduate high school. Then a world war, then home — and
> then he mostly just… stayed. One town. One life.
>
> For about twenty-five years, he pumped gas and fixed cars. Then, instead of retiring, he
> swept floors at the local JCPenney for seventeen more — until he was seventy-six.

**[VISUAL: split-screen inventory of his life, drawn like a museum exhibit with cheerful
labels: COAT (safety pins), CAR (used Toyota Yaris), BREAKFAST (coffee + peanut butter
muffin), PARKING (three blocks away — a coin walks away from a parking meter, offended).]**

> **VO:** Everything you could see about Ronald Read said "poor." A coat repaired with
> safety pins. A second-hand Toyota. He parked blocks out of town to avoid feeding a meter,
> and cut his own firewood into his nineties.
>
> And everyone believed what they could see. For ninety-two years, an entire town filed
> him under "broke."

**[VISUAL: a lawyer's office. The will unrolls like a scroll — and keeps unrolling, out the
door, down the street. The number 8,000,000 assembles itself from tiny coins.]**

> **VO:** Then his will was read: roughly eight million dollars. One-point-two million to
> the local library. Four-point-eight million to the local hospital — the biggest gifts
> either had ever received. From the man whose coffee strangers used to pay for.
>
> So. How does a janitor build eight million dollars? Not with a secret, a lottery ticket,
> or a lucky bet — but with a machine. A machine anyone can build. Let's take it apart.

## PART 2 — "The Gap" (2:30–4:15)

**[VISUAL: a bathtub. Water pours in from a tap labeled INCOME; drains out a hole labeled
SPENDING. The water level barely moves. A tiny janitor bird watches, mop in hand.]**

> **VO:** Part one of the machine: the gap.
>
> Forget everything you associate with the word "rich" — the salary, the job title, the car.
> Wealth is not how much flows in. Wealth is a bathtub. Income pours in through the tap;
> spending drains out the bottom. The only thing that ever accumulates is the difference.
>
> Ronald Read's tap was tiny — a gas station wage, then a part-time janitor's. He never
> earned his way to eight million. But his drain was almost closed. Friends estimated he
> kept — saved — on the order of eighty percent of everything that came in.

**[VISUAL: side-by-side tubs — a golden tub with a firehose tap AND a giant open drain
(level: zero, water spraying everywhere, tiny sports car floating past), next to Read's
rusty tub with a dripping tap and a sealed drain, slowly, steadily filling.]**

> **VO:** This is the part your eyes get wrong. A big tap with a big drain holds nothing.
> A small tap with a sealed drain fills up — slowly, quietly, invisibly. Most people, when
> more water comes in, respond by widening the drain. Read treated the gap itself as the
> thing he owned. The safety pins in his coat weren't poverty. They were policy.
>
> But a full bathtub is just a pile of water. Money sitting still doesn't grow. Which brings
> us to the actual engine.

**[ON-SCREEN, big friendly type: WEALTH = WHAT COMES IN − WHAT GOES OUT]**

## PART 3 — "The Machine" (4:15–6:30)

**[VISUAL: a bank vault opens. Inside: a five-inch stack of paper stock certificates,
drawn like a little skyline. Names flip past: Procter & Gamble, Johnson & Johnson,
American Express, railroads, utilities.]**

> **VO:** Part two: the machine itself.
>
> When Ronald Read passed away, his safe-deposit box held a stack of stock certificates
> five inches thick — at least ninety-five companies. And they were, frankly, the most
> boring companies in America. Soap. Toothpaste. Ketchup. Banks. Railroads. Things he
> could understand from a gas station forecourt.
>
> Boring was the point. Almost all of them paid dividends — a small cash payment,
> just for owning them.

**[VISUAL: a single share drawn as a friendly cell. It pings out a coin. The coin is
swallowed back in — and the cell divides into two cells. Then four. Then eight. Time-lapse:
the screen slowly fills with softly glowing cells, like a petri dish becoming a galaxy.]**

> **VO:** And here's the trick that makes it a machine and not a piggy bank: every dividend
> those companies mailed him went straight back in — buying more shares, which paid more
> dividends, which bought more shares. Money that makes money, that makes money.
>
> You've seen this pattern before. It's how bacteria take over a petri dish. It's how one
> infected cell becomes a pandemic. Biologists call it exponential growth. Investors call
> it compounding. It is the same curve — and it's just as unstoppable when it's working
> *for* you.
>
> Read fed the machine for sixty-five years and did the single hardest thing in finance:
> nothing. Through recessions, oil shocks, Black Monday, the dot-com crash, 2008 — the man
> who fixed cars never once slammed the brakes.

**[VISUAL: one cell in the dish flickers and dies — labeled LEHMAN BROTHERS, 2008.
The dish barely notices. 94 others keep glowing and dividing.]**

> **VO:** And no — he wasn't magically immune to mistakes. One of his ninety-five companies
> was Lehman Brothers. In 2008, it went to zero. Total loss. It didn't matter. One cell
> died; ninety-four kept dividing. That's what diversification is actually for. Not to
> avoid mistakes — to make every mistake survivable.

## PART 4 — "Your Broken Brain" (6:30–8:00)

**[VISUAL: a cute cartoon brain sitting at a desk, confidently drawing a straight line
on a graph. The real curve — flat, flat, flat, then vertical — sneaks up behind it.]**

> **VO:** So why does this feel like a magic trick? Why did an entire town — smart, normal
> people — stare at this man for decades and see nothing?
>
> Because your brain is running ancient software. Humans are built to understand *linear*
> things: walk twice as long, get twice as far. Exponential things break us. We can't feel
> them. We didn't evolve to.
>
> Watch what that means in numbers. Take Read's profile as a rough model — about three
> hundred dollars a month, growing at eight percent a year. After ten years of discipline:
> fifty-five thousand dollars. A used car. This is where almost everyone quits, because a
> decade of sacrifice bought a Toyota.

**[VISUAL: the ladder animates — a bar chart where each bar dwarfs the previous:
$55k… $450k… $1.6M… then the axis has to zoom out twice to fit $8M.]**

> **VO:** But hold on. Thirty years: about four hundred fifty thousand. Forty-five years:
> one-point-six million. Sixty-five years — Ronald Read's actual runway?
>
> Eight million dollars. The same number the lawyer read out in Vermont.
>
> Look at the shape of that. The machine does almost nothing for decades — then everything,
> at the end. The last ten years create more money than the first fifty combined. Compounding
> pays like a slot machine that only ever hits if you never leave the chair. And that —
> right there — is why wealth is invisible. The growth all happens where nobody's looking:
> in the future.
>
> Yes, this is a simplified model — real markets are messier. But the shape of the curve
> is not negotiable. It's math.

**[ON-SCREEN: "Your brain thinks in lines. Money moves in curves."]**

## PART 5 — "The Catch" (8:00–9:15)

**[VISUAL: mood softens. The janitor bird sits alone in a diner with his coffee and muffin.
Out the window, the giant golden curve towers over the town — and he isn't looking at it.]**

> **VO:** Now, an honest story includes the catch. Ronald Read wore safety pins in his coat
> while eight million dollars sat in a vault. He may have run the gap *too* wide. Because
> money isn't really money — it's stored life. Hours you traded for numbers. And hoarding
> all of it is just a different way of never spending your life.
>
> Here's the strangely freeing part. You can't control the market. You can't control what
> you were paid last decade, and you don't get Read's sixty-five years back. Almost
> everything about this game is outside your hands — except two things. The width of your
> gap. And when you start the clock.
>
> Two levers. Both yours. Nobody else's. And the clock lever is the one nobody respects,
> because it does nothing today — and everything later. The best day to start was years ago.
> The second-best day is annoyingly, boringly, today.

**[VISUAL: the two levers appear as actual levers: GAP and CLOCK. A hand — yours,
first-person view — rests on them.]**
