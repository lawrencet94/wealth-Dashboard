## ACT 1 — "The Life" (0:35–3:10)

**[VISUAL: Archivist pulls a drawer labeled WALKER, ANTOINE. Reenactments: a Chicago
playground court at dusk; a college arena in Kentucky blue; a draft-night handshake.]**

> **VO:** Antoine Walker. Raised on Chicago's South Side, one of six children.
> Basketball was the way out, and he took it at full speed: a national championship
> at Kentucky at nineteen, the sixth pick of the 1996 draft, the Boston Celtics.
> By twenty-one, he'd signed a six-year contract worth seventy-one million dollars.
>
> Read that timeline again. At an age when most people are negotiating their first
> salary, Walker was a millionaire seventy times over. As he put it himself, years
> later: the money came fast — the education didn't come with it.

**[VISUAL: montage shot like archive photography, numbered evidence tags: a row of luxury
cars; a jeweler's tray; tailored suits; a mansion gate; a private-jet stairway.]**

> **VO:** And he lived like the money would never stop. He has said he modeled the
> lifestyle on the moguls he admired — the Jay-Zs of the world. Cars, jewelry, tailors,
> tables for twenty. He bought his mother the house he'd promised her as a kid — the
> detail everyone forgets when they tell this story as a cautionary tale. Much of the
> spending wasn't selfish. By his own account, at the peak he was supporting around
> thirty people. One paycheck. Thirty passengers.

**[VISUAL: hard cut — a federal court document on the desk. The words CHAPTER 7 resolve
into focus. Below them, two numbers type themselves out.]**

> **VO:** His last NBA game was in 2008. In May 2010, he filed for Chapter 7 bankruptcy:
> twelve-point-seven million dollars in debts against four-point-three million in assets.
> Even his championship ring went to auction.
>
> One hundred and eight million dollars, and the file closed in the red.
> How? Let's open it.

## ACT 2 — "The Evidence" (3:10–7:15)

**[VISUAL: evidence board. Three tagged folders pinned under string lights:
EXHIBIT A — THE BURN. EXHIBIT B — THE EMPIRE. EXHIBIT C — THE CLIFF.]**

### Exhibit A — The Burn (3:10–4:30)

**[VISUAL: an animated ledger — the mirror image of Episode 1. Left column INCOME — enormous.
Right column SPENDING — grows until it overlaps. The gap between them shrinks, glows red.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit A. The burn. Start with the number nobody quotes: taxes. Of the
> one hundred and eight million, Walker has said fifty-five million went straight
> to the taxman. The real fortune was closer to fifty-three million — still immense,
> but suddenly the story is half as impossible as the headline.
>
> Now run the other side of the ledger. Twelve years of cars that lost value the day
> they were bought, jewelry, gambling — including, by court record, more than a million
> dollars in bad checks to casinos — and thirty people drawing on one income.
> Remember the janitor's lesson: wealth is the gap between what comes in and what
> goes out. Walker's income was five thousand times larger than Ronald Read's.
> His gap was smaller. Sometimes it was negative.

**[ON-SCREEN: "A bigger paycheck is not a bigger gap." — ledger stamp.]**

### Exhibit B — The Empire (4:30–5:55)

**[VISUAL: an architect's model of Chicago row houses, lit amber. Paper deeds stack up
around it. A red line — the year 2008 — sweeps across the model like a shadow.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit B. The empire. Walker didn't only spend — he invested, and this is the
> part of the file that should unsettle you, because on paper he did the responsible thing.
> He put roughly eighteen million dollars into real estate, much of it development back
> home on Chicago's South Side. Properties, projects, loans — many of them personally
> guaranteed. In other words: concentrated, leveraged, and illiquid. All three risks,
> stacked on one bet, in one city, with his own signature on the debt.
>
> Then 2008 arrived. The market didn't just fall — it froze. The properties couldn't
> sell, the loans still could collect, and eighteen million dollars turned into the
> anchor that pulled everything else down. The janitor owned ninety-five small pieces
> of everything. The All-Star owned one big piece of one thing.

**[ON-SCREEN: "Diversification isn't decoration. It's the exit."]**

### Exhibit C — The Cliff (5:55–7:15)

**[VISUAL: the pocket watch again — but this time the compounding curve runs backwards:
a towering column of income that simply stops in 2008, while the spending line keeps going.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit C. The cliff — and this is the exhibit that applies to you even if
> your paycheck has never had six zeroes. An NBA career is a normal working life
> compressed into a decade: Walker's income didn't taper toward retirement, it
> stopped — at thirty-one. The spending, the mortgages, the thirty passengers —
> those didn't stop with it.
>
> From final game to bankruptcy filing took roughly two years. That's the measurement
> that matters: not what he earned, but how long the life he'd built could survive
> without new income. Two years, on a hundred-million-dollar career. The question
> the file asks you is uncomfortable and simple: if your paycheck stopped tomorrow,
> how many months would your file stay open?

**[VISUAL: the backwards curve hits zero and is stamped: RUNWAY — 24 MONTHS.]**

## ACT 3 — "The Verdict" (7:15–9:05)

**[VISUAL: The Archivist closes the folder — then, unusually, opens a second, thinner
folder beside it, marked WALKER, A. — VOL. II.]**

> **VO:** So there it is — but this file has a second volume, and it's the better one.
> Walker didn't disappear. He worked through the bankruptcy, and by 2013 announced
> he was debt-free. Then he did something rarer than earning a hundred million:
> he turned the loss into the product. Today he works in financial education,
> telling this exact story to young athletes so their files read differently.
> He paid a fortune for the lesson. He gives it away for free.
>
> The verdict, then. Income is not wealth — income is weather. Wealth is what you've
> built by the time the weather changes. Ronald Read proved a small income can build
> a fortune. Antoine Walker proved a fortune can't survive without the same habits.
> Same math. Opposite direction.
>
> Verdict: income is a season. Build for winter.

**[ON-SCREEN stamp: VERDICT — INCOME IS A SEASON.]**

## THE LEDGER (9:05–10:30)

**[VISUAL: aesthetic shift — into the Ledger calculator page (screen capture, navy theme).
A "runway" bar chart animates: monthly spending vs. months of savings.]**
**[MUSIC: pulse lifts slightly — resolution, not hype.]**

> **VO:** Now — The Ledger. Your numbers, not his.
>
> Walker's runway was about two years on fifty million after tax. Runway is the same
> equation at every income: what you have saved, divided by what you spend a month.
> On screen: a household spending five thousand a month with ten thousand saved has
> a two-month runway. With thirty thousand saved — six months, the minimum most
> planners want to see. With three hundred thousand invested — five years, and the
> weather starts losing its power over you.
>
> And notice the two levers are the same ones from last week — the gap and the clock —
> just pointed the other way. Every dollar of spending you cut does double work:
> it grows the savings *and* shrinks the monthly number they're divided by.
>
> The runway calculator is in the free Ledger calculators — link below. Put in
> your real numbers and find out how many months your file stays open. And to watch
> that runway grow month by month — your real net worth, tracked across every account,
> with projections tailored to you — our Wealth Garden app is on the App Store,
> also linked below.
>
> None of this is financial advice — it's arithmetic, and one man's very public
> paper trail, told in his own words. Every source is in the description.
>
> Next in the archive: no athletes, no millionaires — your paycheck. Where it actually
