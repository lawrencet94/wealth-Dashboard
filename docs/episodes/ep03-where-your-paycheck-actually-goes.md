## ACT 1 — "The Subject" (0:30–2:50)

**[VISUAL: Archivist pulls a drawer labeled HOUSEHOLD, AVERAGE AMERICAN. Inside: thousands
of identical thin folders stretching back into darkness.]**

> **VO:** Meet the subject. Every year, the Bureau of Labor Statistics runs the Consumer
> Expenditure Survey — thousands of real households recording what they earn and what
> they spend. Not what they *think* they spend. What actually left.
>
> Here is the average American household, twenty twenty-four, in two numbers.
> Income before taxes: one hundred four thousand, two hundred seven dollars.
> Spending: seventy-eight thousand, five hundred thirty-five.

**[VISUAL: the two numbers stack on screen. A gap opens between them, glowing amber —
then tax stamps rain down on it, shrinking it visibly.]**

> **VO:** On paper, that looks like a gap of twenty-five thousand dollars — a
> twenty-four percent savings rate, and this episode should be over. But that income
> number is *before* taxes, and the spending number doesn't include them. Once the
> taxman takes his line, the real gap for the average household shrinks toward
> single digits — and surveys keep finding that a large share of Americans are
> living paycheck to paycheck at every income level, including six figures.
>
> So the mystery isn't whether the money leaves. It's that it leaves *invisibly*.
> The average household can't name where a quarter of it went. Let's open the file.

**[VISUAL: hard cut — the pay stub pinned to the evidence board, red string running
from it to three tagged folders.]**

## ACT 2 — "The Evidence" (2:50–7:00)

**[VISUAL: evidence board. Three tagged folders under string lights:
EXHIBIT A — THE BIG THREE. EXHIBIT B — THE QUIET LEAKS. EXHIBIT C — THE MISSING LINE.]**

### Exhibit A — The Big Three (2:50–4:20)

**[VISUAL: a house, a car, and a dinner plate rendered as amber evidence photographs,
each with a price tag typing itself out.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit A. The big three. Housing: twenty-six thousand, two hundred sixty-six
> dollars a year — a full third of everything the average household spends. Add
> transportation — roughly thirteen thousand — and those two lines alone consume
> half the budget. Half. Before a single meal, prescription, or birthday present.
> Add food, and the big three take roughly six dollars of every ten.
>
> Here is why this exhibit matters: most budget advice attacks the small stuff —
> skip the coffee, cancel a streaming service. But you cannot latte your way out of
> a budget where housing and cars have already taken half. The big three are decided
> a few times a decade — which house, which car, which lease — and each decision
> locks in years of the file. Get the big three right, and the small stuff barely
> matters. Get them wrong, and no amount of coffee discipline saves you.

**[ON-SCREEN: "You can't out-coffee a bad car payment." — ledger stamp.]**

### Exhibit B — The Quiet Leaks (4:20–5:40)

**[VISUAL: the archive room floor — a paper trail of small receipts, each igniting a
faint amber ember as the camera passes: subscriptions, delivery fees, interest lines.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit B. The quiet leaks. Healthcare: over six thousand a year, two-thirds
> of it insurance premiums that ratchet up quietly at renewal. Then the modern leaks —
> the ones the survey's older files never had. Subscriptions that renew themselves.
> Delivery apps that turn a twelve-dollar meal into twenty-four. Buy-now-pay-later
> installments. And interest — the leak that compounds *against* you, the exact
> machine from File Zero-Zero-One, running in reverse.
>
> No single leak sinks the ship. That's what makes them dangerous: each one is too
> small to fight, and together they're why the average household can't account for
> thousands a year. The janitor's ledger had no leaks. He knew where every dollar
> was because every dollar had a job.

**[ON-SCREEN: "Small leaks don't sink ships. Uncounted ones do."]**

### Exhibit C — The Missing Line (5:40–7:00)

**[VISUAL: a ledger page, fully written except one line at the top — blank, highlighted
amber. The camera holds on the empty line.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit C. The missing line — and this is the real finding of the file.
> Look at any household budget and you'll see housing first, because the mortgage
> is due on the first. Everything gets paid in order of who demands it loudest.
> And the one party who never demands anything — future you — gets whatever is
> left over. Usually, that's nothing.
>
> The households that break the pattern all do the same strange thing: they move
> savings from the bottom of the ledger to the top. Pay the missing line *first* —
> automatically, on payday, before the money can be seen — and let the rest of the
> month happen to what remains. A simple frame for it is fifty-thirty-twenty:
> fifty percent to needs, thirty to wants, twenty to the missing line. The exact
> split matters less than the order. What's paid first gets paid. What's paid last
> gets skipped.

**[VISUAL: the blank line fills in: "PAY YOURSELF — 20%" and moves to the top of the page,
stamped: LINE ONE.]**

## ACT 3 — "The Verdict" (7:00–8:50)

**[VISUAL: The Archivist closes the thin folder and holds it beside the two thick ones —
READ and WALKER — from the earlier episodes.]**

> **VO:** So there it is. The paycheck doesn't vanish. It follows instructions —
> written by landlords, lenders, and renewal dates. The average file spends half on
> shelter and wheels, leaks a few thousand through lines nobody reads, and leaves
> the most important line blank, because no one bills you for your own future.
>
> Put this file between the other two and the whole archive so far says one thing.
> The janitor's superpower wasn't frugality — it was that his money had standing
> instructions, and the first instruction was *stay*. The All-Star's downfall wasn't
> the spending itself — it was that a hundred million dollars arrived without any
> instructions at all. Your paycheck is smaller than his and bigger than the
> janitor's early wages. The instructions are the part you control.
>
> Verdict: a paycheck without instructions follows someone else's.

**[ON-SCREEN stamp: VERDICT — WRITE THE INSTRUCTIONS.]**

## Production notes

- Lightest generation load of the first three episodes (~18 shots) — leans on the
  archive library plus heavy dashboard screen capture. Good episode to batch first.
- **Verified figures (BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2024, released Dec 2025):**
  income before taxes $104,207; average expenditures $78,535; housing $26,266 (33%);
  housing + transportation = 50% of spending (transportation ≈ $13k derived);
  healthcare $6,197 ($4,055 insurance + $2,142 medical). Before recording, pull the
  exact food figure from the BLS table and replace "add food… six dollars of every ten"
  if it moves the share by more than a point.
- Ledger math (verified): 20% of $8,684/mo gross ≈ $1,737/mo; FV at 8%/yr ≈ $600k @15y,
  ≈ $2.4M @30y. Script says "over half a million" and "crosses two million" — safe.
- 50/30/20 framing: popularized by Elizabeth Warren in *All Your Worth* (2005) — credit
  in description, not VO.
- Paycheck-to-paycheck claim is deliberately soft ("surveys keep finding a large share") —
  do not attach a specific percentage without pulling a current survey first.
- Tone guardrail: no shame language about spending; the villain is the missing
  instructions, never the viewer.
