## ACT 1 — "The Myth" (0:35–2:45)

**[VISUAL: Archivist pulls a drawer labeled WINDFALLS. Inside, one document sits on top:
a page reading "70% OF LOTTERY WINNERS GO BANKRUPT," photocopied over and over, each
copy blurrier than the last.]**

> **VO:** First, the fact-check — because this archive checks its numbers,
> even the famous ones. The seventy-percent figure is usually credited to the National
> Endowment for Financial Education. Trace it back and here's what you find: a
> think-tank discussion in 2001, where a participant apparently said it out loud —
> once, unsourced. It was quoted, then the quote was quoted, then the quote of the
> quote was quoted, for twenty years.
>
> In 2018, the organization itself put out a statement saying, in effect: this number
> is not ours, it is not supported by any research we've ever done, please stop.
> The internet did not stop.

**[VISUAL: the photocopied page gets stamped in red: UNVERIFIED — DISAVOWED BY SOURCE, 2018.]**

> **VO:** Why does a fake number survive twenty years of repetition? Because it flatters
> the people repeating it. *They* couldn't handle millions, but you could — you've
> thought about it in the shower, you have a plan. The myth turns other people's
> windfalls into a morality tale and lets everyone else off the hook.
>
> The real records don't let anyone off the hook. Let's open them.

## ACT 2 — "The Evidence" (2:45–7:05)

### Exhibit A — The Real Numbers (2:45–4:00)

**[VISUAL: a wall of thin folders — thousands of them — labeled FLORIDA, 1993–2001.
A counter ticks up: 35,000 WINNERS.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit A. The real numbers. In the 2000s, three economists did what nobody
> quoting the myth ever had: they took roughly thirty-five thousand actual Florida
> lottery winners and matched them, name by name, against bankruptcy court records.
> The study is called "The Ticket to Easy Street," and its headline finding is almost
> boring: fewer than six percent of winners filed for bankruptcy within five years.
>
> Not seventy percent. Six. About the same rate as lottery *players* who won little
> or nothing — which makes sense, because people who play heavily are, on average,
> already under financial strain. The ticket didn't ruin them. It found them.

**[ON-SCREEN: "70%: invented. Real figure: <6% in 5 years." — ledger stamp.]**

### Exhibit B — The Postponement (4:00–5:30)

**[VISUAL: two rows of folders — SMALL WINNERS ($150–$10,000) and BIG WINNERS
($50,000–$150,000). Timeline bars grow out of each toward a red BANKRUPTCY marker;
the big winners' bar starts later — but reaches the same place.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit B — and this is where the file turns strange. The economists compared
> people who won small prizes against people who won fifty to a hundred and fifty
> thousand dollars — real, debt-clearing money. In the first two years, the big
> winners were only about half as likely to go bankrupt. The money helped. Of course
> it did.
>
> Then years three through five arrived — and the big winners caught up completely.
> Same bankruptcy rate as the small winners, just delayed. And the detail that should
> stop you cold: by the time they filed, the winnings were essentially gone — the money
> hadn't been converted into assets, or debt paid down, or anything that lasted.
>
> Read that as the archive reads it: a windfall didn't change the trajectory.
> It paid to *postpone* the trajectory. The leak from File Zero-Zero-Three was never
> patched — the winners just poured water in faster for a while. Money buys time.
> It does not, by itself, buy change.

**[ON-SCREEN: "The jackpot didn't fix the file. It delayed it."]**

### Exhibit C — The Pattern (5:30–7:05)

**[VISUAL: a single folder opens: ADAMS, EVELYN. Reenactment, respectful and unglamorous:
a New Jersey storefront, 1985; a second winning ticket, 1986; casino lights reflected
in a window; later, a modest trailer at dusk, lit warmly.]**

> **VO:** Exhibit C. The pattern, in one remarkable life. Evelyn Adams won the New
> Jersey lottery in 1985 — and then won it *again* in 1986. Two jackpots, about
> five and a half million dollars combined, back when that bought a great deal more
> than it does now. Within years, it was gone — to Atlantic City tables, and to an
> endless line of people asking. She said it herself, in interviews she gave openly:
> everybody wanted her money, everybody had a hand out, and she couldn't say no.
> Her words: she was a big fool.
>
> Hold her file up to the light and you can see every mechanism the researchers found.
> Windfall dollars *feel* different from earned dollars — economists call it mental
> accounting; money that arrives without work gets spent without weight. The social
> pressure is real and relentless — a public windfall turns every relationship into
> a quiet invoice. And underneath both: the missing line from File Zero-Zero-Three.
> A windfall is a paycheck with no instructions — the biggest one you'll ever get,
> handed to whatever habits were already running.
>
> Even winning twice wasn't enough. Because the second jackpot arrived at the same
> habits as the first.

**[ON-SCREEN: "A windfall funds your habits. It doesn't change them."]**

## ACT 3 — "The Verdict" (7:05–8:55)

**[VISUAL: The Archivist lays the windfall folder open beside WALKER's. The two files
mirror each other on the desk.]**

> **VO:** Notice this file is Antoine Walker's, at every scale. His hundred and eight
> million was a windfall too — it just arrived over twelve seasons instead of one
> ticket. Same speed of money, same missing instructions, same crowd at the door,
> same two-year cliff after the income stopped. The lottery is simply the purest
> laboratory for the thing this archive keeps finding: sudden money doesn't rewrite
> your ledger. It runs your existing ledger at maximum volume.
>
> And that's why this episode matters even if you never buy a ticket — because
> windfalls come for almost everyone eventually, in ordinary clothes. An inheritance.
> A severance package. A house sale. A signing bonus. The research on those looks
> just the same: money that arrives faster than instructions, leaves faster than
> planned.
>
> The good news is the fix is known, and it's the same one the janitor and the
> secretary ran on their small, slow windfalls every payday: instructions first,
> money second.
>
> Verdict: a windfall is an amplifier, not an answer.

**[ON-SCREEN stamp: VERDICT — AMPLIFIER, NOT ANSWER.]**

> **VO:** Now — The Ledger. Your numbers, and a protocol you'll hopefully need someday.
>
> First, the math that would have saved most of the names in this file: a million
> dollars is not a fortune — it's a salary. At a sustainable four-percent drawdown,
> one million produces about forty thousand a year, for life. Life-changing?
> Absolutely. "Never work again, buy the cars, carry the entourage"? Not even close.
> Most winners spend from the million. The ones who keep it spend from the forty.
>
> Second, the protocol — the standing instructions to write *before* the money knows
> your name. One: tell almost no one. Two: do nothing for six to twelve months — park
> it somewhere safe and boring and let the shock pass. Three: hire advice that is paid
> by fee, not by commission. Four: convert it into the two levers this archive keeps
> stamping — a wider gap, and a longer clock.
>
> The free Wealth Dashboard — link below — has the windfall view: enter any lump sum
> and see the income it sustains, and the runway it buys at your real monthly spend.
> Run your inheritance, your bonus, your imaginary jackpot. Write the instructions now.

> None of this is financial advice — it's arithmetic, thirty-five thousand court

>
